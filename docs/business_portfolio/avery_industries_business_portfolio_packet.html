Ethical AI Positioning

Avery Industries is building ATLAS around a rights-aware AI philosophy: creator-owned data, human approval, asset-rights tracking, and transparent governance.

Ethical AI thesis

ATLAS is being positioned as an AI system built around consent, provenance, ownership, and human approval. The company goal is to make AI useful without treating artists, musicians, software developers, game developers, writers, or creators as disposable input sources.

Founder-created training foundation

Cole Avery serves as CEO and AI engineer, building ATLAS around founder-created systems, company-authored workflows, internal documentation, owned IP, and approved materials. This reduces dependence on exploitative or unclear creative datasets.

Creative-rights operating model

  • Artist, developer, and musician rights are tracked before public or commercial use.
  • Client assets are not reused without permission.
  • Unknown rights block public/commercial deployment.
  • Generated assets are labeled, reviewed, and routed through approval gates.

Practical ethics, not slogans

  • No automated publishing, spending, customer messaging, grant submission, or contracts without Cole approval.
  • No unsupported public claims.
  • No medical, legal, financial, funding, or income guarantees.
  • No fake engagement, testimonials, reviews, or customers.
Diligence-safe claim: ATLAS should be described as an Ethical AI system by design and governance. Claims such as “the most ethical AI” should be reserved for future third-party validation, audits, or published evidence.

Sustainable Compute and Data Center Strategy

ATLAS should grow without causing avoidable global, environmental, or financial harm.

Global-friendly data center roadmap

Avery Industries’ long-term infrastructure plan is to avoid growth that creates unnecessary financial or environmental harm. The roadmap prioritizes efficient workloads, renewable power, right-sized compute, and distributed processing before large centralized expansion.

Solar-first infrastructure

  • Future data center capacity should be paired with solar farms or renewable-energy agreements where practical.
  • Compute expansion should be tied to actual revenue, user need, and mission impact.
  • Energy usage should be measured and reported before scale decisions.

Cooling innovation plan

The company will research low-impact cooling options before committing to large facilities, including passive cooling, liquid cooling, heat reuse, modular thermal design, and location-aware data center planning.

Downsizing the data center

Avery’s preferred long-term direction is to reduce centralized infrastructure pressure by moving appropriate workloads toward affordable local devices: home security hubs, home data centers, and personal servers that can provide private processing power at the edge.

Infrastructure thesis: scale compute by making it cleaner, smaller, more distributed, and more user-owned before defaulting to massive centralized data centers.
